What great support looks like here

Tone, empathy, and ownership are what actually make the difference between a ticket that gets closed and a player who comes back — for our current base, and for everyone we're bringing onto Splash for the first time.

Protecting the people giving that empathy

Great support runs in both directions. Reps are asked to bring real empathy to every interaction — the team also has to protect the people doing that work, especially as ticket volume grows.

Sustainable pace

A few things that are true here, not just nice to say:

  • If a ticket needs escalation, a user is being rude or abusive, or a user asks for a supervisor or manager, loop in Abbey — you're not expected to absorb it and handle it alone.
  • No rep deserves to be treated poorly by a user. Being professional doesn't mean tolerating abuse — loop Abbey in and let her take it from there.
  • A hard ticket doesn't have to follow you into the next one — resetting between difficult tickets is part of doing the job well, not a break from it.
  • If hostility from the same user keeps showing up, that's a potential account disable/closure conversation, not something a rep should have to manage alone, repeatedly. Please loop in Abbey.

Where support is headed in 2026

As paid contests grow, Splash is moving routine questions — withdrawals, verification, contest outcomes — away from manual digging and toward answers pulled straight from real account data through Fin. It's not about removing the human element. It's about freeing reps to spend judgment and empathy on the tickets that actually need it, instead of the ones that just need a fact.

Old model→New model
When a bot hands off to a human

As Fin takes on more of the routine questions, there's one clear line: the moment a player expresses frustration twice, or asks something outside Fin's scope, a human takes over — no exceptions. Automation is meant to win back time on the easy questions. It should never cost a frustrated player a real person's attention.

Before you post — every escalation needs this

Posting requirements

Whichever channel you're posting in, include all of the following so the team can review in a timely manner — a post missing any of these just bounces back with a "can you also send..." and costs everyone time:

  • UID
  • The issue
  • Troubleshooting steps already done
  • Screenshots — attach them, or get them before posting
  • Any other detailed information that helps the team troubleshoot quickly

03

Code of conduct & professionalism

How we carry ourselves in every channel — with players, with each other, and with the platform's name and legal standing attached to what we say.

The core rule

The player never experiences silence as your first response. Send an outbound message before you ping T&S, post in Slack, or snooze a ticket.

The terminology rule

We never use the word "pool" on Splash. Some states classify pools as a game of skill and others as gambling, so we exclusively say "contest" to stay compliant across jurisdictions.
